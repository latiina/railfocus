Rail Focus needs YOUR help

As I have explained elsewhere, Rail Focus is very much a one-man, hobby project. There is no large group of people behind it, nor is there any kind of funding - just me!

But everyone reading this can help, by passing details of this newsletter onto friends and family, or colleagues who may be interested, and by clicking on any of the adverts that interest you. Editorial
Welcome to this, the first edition of Rail Focus. The aim behind this project is to fulfil an interest of mine, to try out different ways of working and to hopefully provide an interesting read to people, as well as a selection of images, roughly every two to three weeks.

I have had an interest in the UK national network for many years, and to a smaller extend heritage steam and European railways, especially within Germany. While this email version of a blog will concentrate on the National Network within the Great Britain, the other areas of my interests may make an appearance from time to time. My aim is not to try and be a big magazine like you would buy from the newsagents, more to focus on a few stories and small features, told in my own words and photos, that I think may interest people. There is a link further down on the right that will allow you to pass comments back, or perhaps suggest ideas for future stories/features.

So how do you obtain Rail Focus and read it? Well firstly, it's free! There's nothing to pay (although there is a donation button if you want to say thanks with a few quid!) so at its very basic level you could just come onto the website every so often and read the current and previous editions. Alternatively, you could register your email address and every time a new edition is published, it will arrive on your email, so you don't need to visit the site at all. As a note, I have put a privacy policy on the site, but in essence, if you sign up to receive Rail Focus, your email address is used just for that - it's not sold or give to anyone else, and you won't get bombarded with emails from me, either.

I do want to make clear that this is very much a one-man hobby project, whether it grows beyond that in the future is anyone's guess, but while I am aiming to produce an update roughly fortnightly, those timescales may vary as we go through the year. However, I do need help from you, the readers. Please pass reference about Rail Focus onto your friends and colleagues, and help me to spread the word and increase the number of people reading this each time. You can click here for some graphics if you want to help by putting them on your website. Each issue will contain ads from Google (yes, I know not everyone likes them) but despite being digital, there are some costs incurred through sending emails, hosting the website etc, so if you see an advert that interests you, please to click on it and explore more. Each click gains me a few pence of income, all of which will be used to support this venture, so the more ads that are clicked on, the more I can do. Should any organisation or individual wish to advertise in, or sponsor an edition, please do contact me through the website - I'm always happy to talk.

That's about it for this editorial, I hope you enjoy the content, and welcome your feedback. If anyone wants to send any pictures in, please do so, I've put some details on helping on the website (click here), and I'll try and use them where I can. Thanks for reading, and I hope you enjoy Rail Focus.
Headlight
Further down in this edition, I have taken a look at how the Rail Head Treatment Trains have changed over the years, so it is perhaps a good opportunity to start with a picture of one of this year’s workings. Those trains operating out of York usually make use of Class 20 locomotives, which have a low axle weight, because of some restrictions en route. Here 20302 is joined by 20312 as they power through Althorpe, near Scunthorpe. The lifting bridge, called the King George V bridge, is one that dates from 19xx, and I always think makes a nice backdrop to an image. The bridge celebrated its ima100th anniversary last year and was designed to lift to allowing sailing barges on the River Trent to pass underneath.

How the Great Western electrification is progressing

Each day, South West Trains operates over 1,600 trains across its suburban and main line networks, stretching from London Waterloo, through the London suburbs into Devon and Dorset.

The vast majority of those operations are formed of electric multiple units from Classes 444, 450, 455, 456 or 458, with the remainder being diesel worked with Class 158s or 159s from Salisbury Depot. While Northam, near Southampton, looks after the Siemens Class 444 and 450 fleets, the remaining three classes are all under the watchful eye of Wimbledon Traincare Depot, as far as exams and maintenance are concerned.

The stabling facility at Wimbledon was first created by the London & South Western Railway (LSWR), the precursor to the Southern Railway as a stabling and cleaning base for its fleet of Electric Multiple Units, but the site has a closer affiliation with the EMU network than most. Part of the current depot, was originally the location of Durnsford Road Power Station, a facility built by the LSWR around the early 1900s, shortly before the start of the First World War, to provide electrical power to the network. The boilers here were fed from a high-level bunker line, with coal wagons (that had arrived loaded by rail) being propelled up the concrete ramp by small Bo-Bo electric locomotives. The power station, concrete ramp and location of the main line, as it was in 1924, can be seen in this view on the ‘Britain from Above’ website.

Until the development of Wimbledon Depot in the early 1970s, Durnsford Road Depot was an eight road facility, located around the area where the new bogie drop is now located. A fairly simple affair, it allowed the outstabling of units during the day, or between duties. In April 1974 the new Wimbledon Depot opened as part of the Regional Berthing and Maintenance Plan created by what was then British Rail’s Southern Region. Built on the land of the former power station, and bringing about the demolition of the old Durnsford Road shed, it result in the creation of dedicated undercover maintenance, repair and servicing sheds, along with stabling facilities, the latter of which were also created at Staines.

Today, the site employs 198 members of staff, from the management team down to fitters and cleaners, who are responsible for the delivery into service of safe, maintained and clean trains. In total 91 Class 455 four-car, 24 Class 456 two-car and 36 Class 458/5 five-car EMUs are allocated to Wimbledon and each weekday, the depot is required to provide 82, 20 and 32 sets respectively, for service, a tall order that shows how heavily SWTs unit fleet is diagrammed. Of the nine Class 455s that are not required for service, two will be located at Bournemouth Depot at anyone time for their second C6 exam, which consists of a heavy overhaul including a complete repaint, three sets will be at Eastleigh Works for AC traction conversion and depending on where in the maintenance cycle the sets are there could be another unit at Bournemouth for a C4 exam, which basically deals with bogies, underframes, wheels and couplings. The remaining three/four sets are therefore available to Wimbledon for planned or corrective maintenance.

The heavy maintenance was moved to the expanded depot at Bournemouth following the creation of spare capacity as a result of the Class 442 work being lost a few years ago. SWT to took the decision to invest in the depot as it strategic long-term overhaul facility, which included the installation of a dedicated, purpose-built paintshop. While the movement of units to and fromthe depot incurs a small cost and manpower requirement (i.e. The driver) it remains a more cost-effective option than employing a larger workshop to undertake the necessary tasks.

Wimbledon is also handling a number of local modifications to its fleet. With the Class 458 EMUs, this involves the fitment of WiFi facilities, with around half of the units now been fitted. The Class 455s are being fitted with expanded stand hoppers, to allow them to be refilled from within the train.

With the exception of the new bogie drop building, the layout of the depot is fairly similar to the condition in which it was built in 1973/74. The depot site is divided into a north end and south end as a result of Durnsford Road. The south end handles the main day-to-day maintenance and repair aspects of the site, with the maintenance shed here comprising of seven single ended roads, two of which (Nos. 12 and 13 roads) are four-car length and being equipped with lifting facilities, form the depots lift shop. Roads 11-9 are eight-car length and have pits to allow low-level and underneath access. Work will shortly commence to extend these roads to 10-car length to allow the new Siemens Class 707 ‘Desiro City’ vehicles to be maintained. The work is expected to take around 12-months to complete, and involves lengthening the roads by one-vehicle length at both ends of the building, due to the restrictions of the site. Roads 7 and 8 are the longest, being able to accommodate 12-car sets, again with pit access along the entire length. Outside the maintenance sheds are a further five roads (Nos 1-6) that provide stabling, with two of these roads also giving the ability to empty the on-board retention tank toilet system. It should be noted that the installation of the toilet emptying system resulted in the removal of Road 3.

Recently, the new bogie drop facility has been installed which has removed a significant workload from the lift shop, allowing the latter to concentrate on other maintenance requirements. The bogie facility allows SWT to ‘drop’ the bogies, wheelsets or traction motors out of a unit for attention. The building is also fitted with an in-situ wheel lathe as part of the bogie drop.

At the north end of the site, nearest to Wimbledon station and adjacent to the flyover, is the depot servicing area, which concentrates purely on cleaning and preparation of units ready for their next working. Roads 1-2 are known as the ‘transfer roads’ and temporarily hold units waiting to move from the Yard along to the Depot for further attention. Road 3 deals with retention tank emptying and roads 4-9 are covered accommodation roads within the covered accommodation, allowing cleaners safe and easy access to the units. Finally, roads 10-24 are purely for berthing units awaiting their next duty.

The depot operates 24 hours a day, seven days a week, and during the day time, will generally be dealing with pre-planned maintenance, such as A, B or C exams, on the day of our visit, (45)5871 was present for attention to a cab-to-cab defect. A further pair of Class 455s, Nos. (45)5860 and (45)5717 had been allocated to a short morning peak-hour working only, to allow them to visit the depot for attention to a loss of power report from the previous day, before being returned to traffic for the evening rush-hour. However with set utilisation and the current exam situation giving two spare units, the decision was taken to hold them on Depot that evening, using the two spare sets to cover the allocated diagram, there by giving engineers at Wimbledon more time to diagnose and correct the fault.

While the bogie drop and wheel lathe facility is primarily used by SWT EMU vehicles, and little outside work is undertaken, there is a regular pathway in the system from Salisbury Depot, allowing the transfer of SWT Class 158 and 159 vehicles to Wimbledon for wheelset attention, on an ‘as required’, but invariably weekly, basis. A production meeting is held at 08:15 each morning, with a run through of which units are stopped and the work being carried out, the expected time for each set to return to traffic and also looking at an overview of the current workload. Twice a day, at 06:00 and 18:00 an update of pre-allocated work from the maintenance controllers in the Waterloo Integrated Control Centre (WICC) is sent through, although discussions are ongoing throughout the day between the Depot and the WICC with regards to this work. Diagramming and programming of sets for exams, especially Class 455 work, is a highly time sensitive job. Arrival of a set before the Depot is ready means that not only is the fleet potentially a set short for the allocated number required in traffic each day, but it is also sat on depot unnecessarily while arrival of a set too late means that the engineering staff allocated to that work could be sat around with nothing to do, so the accurate planning of new arrivals, and release from previous exams can make a huge difference to the smooth running of things.Overnight work will concentrate on cleaning and servicing, along with running repairs and general day-to-day checks and short exams, that can be quickly completed during the late evening/early hours of the morning downtime.

While the workload at Wimbledon is unlikely to change significantly in the coming years where the existing fleet is concerned, big changes are ahead to accommodate the new Class 707 EMUs, now being built by Siemens in Germany. As well as the extension of Roads 9-11 in the Maintenance Shed mentioned earlier, a new stores building is being constructed on site to hold spares, and berthing roads 21-27 are being extended from eight to 10-car roads. The addition of new ‘707’ work at Wimbledon will bring added pressure to the depot workload, which is already at capacity overnight. Part of the ability to ‘add-in’ the ‘707’ servicing is the conversion of the Class 455s from DC to AC traction equipment which has provided a significant increase in reliability and allowed the maintenance plan to move from a cycle based around 10,000 miles between exams, to 15,000. This reduction in workload means that Wimbledon then has the capacity to accept the new ‘Desiro City’ trains, which themselves will operate 20,000 miles between exams.

Out of the 30 five-car units being built, once fully commissioned, a total of 27 will be required for public services every day, with the first expected to go into traffic in mid-April next year, likely as a pair forming a 10-car set. While the ‘707s’ are due to start arriving in the country in mid-November, they will be commissioned through Three Bridges and Clapham Junction Yard initially. The first six sets will be required to undertake 5,000 miles of trouble-free operation before being handed over to SWT, while the remaining 24 will have to undertaken 1,000 miles of trouble-free test running.

Useful Websites
Each edition of Rail Focus, this part of the content is likely to change, and some may be photographic based, others might look at useful websites that I have come across, or interesting books that I think readers may find useful. Some editions may even combine two or more of those, in other words this is the ‘lucky dip’ section!

I am always amazed at those useful little websites that we stumble across, while looking for something else, so this is the opportunity to share the odd ones with the rest of you! These are just my personal choices, there is no paid promotion for these sites, just online places that I have found.

ABRail.co.uk
Im sure many people, like me, back in the day had a number book for marking off those items of rolling stock that had been ‘coped’ or photographed. In today’s digital world ABRail.co.uk is broadly the same thing, only kept much more up to date. Covering locos, carraiges and multiple units, it has provided a go-to reference for me on many occasion.
www.abrail.co.uk


DataRail.co.uk
Until recently, I was an avid follower of RealTimeTrains, for finding out where trains are, or whether a freight is running late. Its still good, and provides a great service but I have recently found a new site - DataRail.
www.datarail.co.uk
